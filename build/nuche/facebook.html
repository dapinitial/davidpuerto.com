Facebook.

Full-stack UX Design


for FBIDE team

  • UX Design
  • Product Strategy
  • UI Development




Contracted as a UI Specialist to research the current FBIDE product and identify pain points and areas to enhance the UI to enhance the overall user experience by QAing the current incarnation of the product, increase discoverability of features as they became available, and creating visual design assets to create a look and feel synonomous with the fidelity that a Facebook product deserves, syntax and UI themes, and finally implementation.





The problem you are trying to solve — What can’t they do now or can’t do well that your design is meant to address?



FBIDE was popular in Facebook Engineering Bootcamp the twelve week springboard to joining an Engineering at the Facebook and where development skills were honed for the rigors of producing products that were touching in some respects over a billion users and counting.


After bootcamp many people left the product. Why? This is what I set out to learn about and produce measurable results as we produced solutions based on evidence-based research: "the use of prior research in a systematic and transparent way to inform a new study so that it is answering questions that matter in a valid, efficient and accessible manner".


To begin we ensured that we have places to document internally and correspond with our users. This came in the internal FB WIKI and through Secret Groups targeting our users.



Users — [Facebook Engineers and Data Scientists. Eventually the public, open-source community, web developers, designers, data scientists, kids learning to code.]









After setting up our sandbox for correspondance and documentation I tasked myself to reach out to the users and created personas identifying people by name, job title, team, responsibilities, etc. All of my initial users were Facebook engineers and data scientists. As this project grew in scope to the point of open sourcing we looked at potential users from designers who used code to young students and hoobyists.









To identify the users to query I used SCUBA to deep dive into Facebook data to determine who were the most active users and then ran queries over the last twelve months to determine who had been an active user that was no longer using FBIDE to determine where we were failing and how we could win them back.


From there I set up web chats with users from various engineering teams and data scientists with varying usage over the last year some were power users and some left it for dead when they left boot camp. I organized a Facebook group and began correspondances.


To announce my presence and what we were doing I created flyers and placed them in common areas and even in the bathrooms with the urinals. I was the Bathroom DJ and I played the music to lessen the burder of the bodily noises that were expressed -- but that's another story.


I began to hang out with the engineers and was using a myriad of IDEs that they enjoyed using began learning to write tests and understand the why behind how engineers are working. It was not only insightful, it was enlightening. I developed a deep appreciation and respect, a camaraderie so-to-speak with engineers across the globe. I was even shouted out on Facebook Video by engineers who were in some respects idols and rocks star developer advocates within the company. People were really excited about what we were working on, Engineers like Kent Beck who people studied about in CompSci classes was on staff and praised my advances by name in one such video. It was humbling as more and more Facebook employees began to know David Puerto as friend of the developers and their advocated their best interests from the Design Lens.





Some of the unexpected findings were that there were a lot of features already in development that were buried or just had not been released during the beta releases of the IDE that were demoed in early bootcamps where they tried to get some more developer interest and participation to create the best cloud-based IDE in the wild.


Bringing the users back was as simple as showing them what was possible and turning them onto the sleek new interface that allowed for their workflow to remain consistent and not impeeded by a UI. Many developers are straight up typing magic. They know it as well as anyone knows any language. It is amazing to watch them work.





I noticed a lot of time was spent jumping back and forth between terminal tabs, build systems, IDEs, terminals Gitub, Phabricator, all sorts of ticketing and management systems and of course Facebook because who needs Slack when you have Facebook Gold? These observations culiminated in feature requests that we spec'd as a team in charge of our own destiny and be began to assemble the ground work that was led by Design that influenced the engineering tools. I had some Product Designers joshing that I was designing the DevTool but they were seeing the results and witnessing the coolness coming out of our team as well as the response from the developers internally and on the team that we were moving by leaps and bounds in the right direction. I even had the company rocking the cool t-shirt. We were buzzing from Seattle to Tel Aviv and not just off the secret in the globe.




I decided to build in-line commenting into the Diff View where engineers view differences between code to see what changed between one commit and another. Super powerful feature as is... but now we decided to pivot, because, what if you were slacking off in Facebook and you committed some code that botched the MobilePerformance in the labratory and 16 of the 300 devices that your code was running on slowed performance? What if we could automate the review process and your manager or whoever the code reviewer was received a Facebook Alert that someone's code blew up something somewhere? What if they recevied that alert in their own Facebook, could click that alert, jump into the code right in facebook, see the DiffView, compare the code in question identify an area for refactor, and then could inline comment in Facebook fashion to: "Slow down and fix your shit!" Then the developer who needed to redo their work would receive an update on their end, could jump back into their code, fix it and commit it and push it for a pull request all in the same work flow in the same window? After we implemented this and began to measure how long it took on average for people to complete these tasks we were able to show how this process improvement alone was saving on average 6 hours of developer time each week. Times thousands of developers. Times the amount of money each one is being paid per hour. You do the math. It's in the millions that feature was saving alone.


Another problem our cloud-based zero-install IDE sought to solve was remotely debugging other people's sandboxes. This was accomplished and implemented and still is one of the best features for this product... RIP.





We were developing themes and we had a listener to inform us of what people were using for their preferred themes and were able to narrow it down to four themes. Then I created our own Light and Dark theme to really enhance our UI because, well, that's a place I enjoy geeking out on. I'm a visual Designer too.











This was an incredibly fun project to begin as FBIDE has ~1000 active users who are internally using the cloud-based IDE to develop and debug code on the HHVM in Hack and best of all languages are supported. FBIDE is demoed to boot campers and interns alike. I found that many people when I started the project had tried it out and switched back to their preferred methods of programming. Upon inquiry I learned that many VIM and eMacs users were going to be hard to convert... unless of course we have VIM and VIM-style key mapping!! So we entered that as a task and delivered! Observing the entire Facebook developer life cycle I was able to extract the basis from which move developers begin and enter and continue and finish programs and their overall workflow including expected feedback loops. Having an active user base and users who were active in our internal Facebook group made for an amazing UX experience querying users and responding to comments. When I started to implement some of the changes literally hundreds of posts were made with such excitement for the enhanced UI experience. I've never been on a project where the users were currently dogfooding the product and it definitely enhanced the outcome by ensuring that every step we made was in the right direction with our users Q&Aing the product for us so we could establish what was working and what was not.



















































































The work from Lyric is now being ported over to another open-source IDE and being co-developed internally with another group called `Nuclide` that I am currently on contract with. We are contributing directly to the GitHub Atom core. Today the entire experience is serving as a living prototype that the users are still using while we put the polish on Nuclide being demoed at F8.


Update. After contributing to GitHub's Atom Core, GitHub's Atom won open-source product of the year. Nuclide is now retired. There is a long and taried history. A lot of political drama, and even a hackathon project, Atom in Orbit, that I distinctly remember suggesting be the direction we go to find a happy medium, keep FBIDE code named Lyric growing in the right direction, and satisfying open-source by redoing it in JavaScript. We just needed to port Atom from the Electron app back to the browser. It was all DOM. No biggie... Oh well. Such is the life in the software game.
