Nordstrom

Let's View

"Leave It Better Than You Found It"

Client: Nordstrom - Seattle, WA
Roles: User Interface, Information Architecture, Visual design & UX
Fields: Fashion, Retail, Lifestyle

Browse My Process Watch UAT Project Video

Problems

When I was contracted to join an engineering team that had big plans to modernize several systems along the PRICE tool chain I was excited. For over six months an internal Enterprise UX team had been dismissive of all requests to help this struggling PRICE org with their modernization efforts on a myriad of products. An organization of designers allocated to specifically address these concerns and issues basically refused to work with my manager. This was opportunity to show what I was made of and that I could drive results, innovate, and deliver on UX. I was coming out of a two-year hiatus that took me from Cape Town to Japan to Colombia, spreading myself around the USA Canada and abroad, spending the equity [recklessly] that I reclaimed after selling the house that I owned for almost a decade. It was time to kick off the ring rust and return to the work force. I was initially tasked with The Off Price Clearance Tool’s “Gone Fishing Experience” and along the way addressed some CSS bugs. In doing so the familiar hodgepodge of CSS drew my desire to not only rewrite the code base but also to also address some UX issues that I noticed. I wireframed and prototyped a new feature demonstrating the aggregation of Errors and Warnings into a console that would serve as our proposal to introduce the foundation for the PRICE Tools Modernization effort on the UI front. I envisioned a multi-tool that would enable authorized users to work across multiple angles of the business via a singular Progressive Web App with a familiar toolset based off a pattern library that other dev teams could draw inspiration from and contribute to. Common UI emerged and we began to cross-pollinate, breaking down walls between engineering teams tackling new projects such as CompIntel and the future FULL Price Clearance Tool, Ticketing, Events, and began to break open silos as a wealth of information from the business began to flow through our applications.

Discovering

When the CompIntel effort began we met with the “power users” of the competitive intelligence process and learned how QAs and Buying Office operated as a unit verifying third-party “Boomerang Data” and the matching and validation of items for the entire catalog were determined for price matching and remaining competitive. There was a lot to learn and we wondered if our tool would actually enhance productivity. Some of the power users were incredibly nimble using traditional Excel spreadsheets, Slack, and email. One thing we knew though that the process of persisting data would be expedited from thirty minutes or more to almost instantaneously. Huge win. Further more, since Boomerang data wasn’t trusted because of the latency between getting the data and being able to validate hundreds of thousands of items, sizes, colors, permutations in styles, colors, cosmetics, household goods, etc, seemed improbable at best. The opportunity to introduce AI to help this process along was on the brink. Perhaps saving millions a month on this third-party service we might be able to remove from the equation. Huge win. One of the issues was definitely tackling how Amazon obfuscates their front-end. I proposed a Chrome Extension with web-sockets that would help the user to identify the drop down and from that select box we could garner what permutations existed using the event.currentTarget to see what unfolded. Huge win as, as we were demoed, bra sizes can be plentiful! Huge wins on time to reduce the amount of laborious manual clicking and recording and verifying and validating that had to be performed by Nordstrom daily!

Ideation & Wireframing

Pivoting off The experience of learning from the users how they contribute to the Nordstrom business daily had my mind turning. The lead engineers and myself spent time talking about the problems and proposing ideas. We spent a lot of time graffiti-ing up whiteboards, pretty much the only reason I even have a job. I love scribbling and drawing pictures. We determined that there were essentially three experiences. Admin for setting up Daily Assignments for users and Departments and Divisions they were responsible for and the Daily and Weekly Cadence. I had big ideas here. I began to examine them on the whiteboard and in Sketch and passed it around with some engineers and we showed some colleagues, and then I immediately began prototyping in CodePen and developing out the CSS and HTML and created higher fidelity clickable prototypes that influenced our overall interface design approach and eventually design system. Working in this manner allowed me to fork and fail often, work in isolation and include the entire landscape to see how things flowed together and built on top of previous UI. It allowed the Common UI to emerge organically and failing and pivoting often and being able to dabble nimbly in the CodePen environment allowed for sharing and testing and demoing across systems and platforms from Mac with retina to older PCs, laptops, and seeing our product on a myriad of screens enabled more transparency and informed design to transpire. We also determined there was a QA and a BO marriage happening and we began to deep dive into questions for AuthZ and whether users were capable of re-assigning and performing both duties. I began to wireframe in Sketch while working in CodePen, which I prefer for all ends of the design process. While I was working on this I was thinking in generalities for Common UI but also how I could add some pizzazz to CompIntel and flex my product design, visual design, interaction design, UI design, and CSS muscles. I was dreaming up animations and microinteractions and features to increase productivity and began to think how we could incentivize our users to be competitive with each other. I wanted to gamify the experience and after hiring a junior design to groom to take over some future projects and basically work the way I do while being closer to the PM PdM side of the UX equation I sent him to the QA who demo’d us the bras and I snuck in some BATMAN FIGHTWORDS into the UI so that I could surprise and delight our QA user. He admitted to being disgusted but she loved it and thought the POW! Zing! Kaboom! BAM! Was “SOO COOL!” as she completed tasks. Easter Eggs were born. Users could see progress and who was the most efficient and performance could not only be measured and assets and inventory reallocated but high performers would be rewarded with discounts or gift cards, extra time off, etc.

Feedback

When we demo’d we didn’t have much push back. I think I nailed the visual appeal that they didn’t even realize they were getting and deserving of... When we demo’d to the VPs we heard it was “The best fucking thing that had ever come out of the org...” and during UAT we uncovered from UX failures that I should have covered but we put this whole thing together in just over a quarter so the timelines were narrow and we were heads down designing, developing, and pracitcing a hybrid super lean UX and dogfooding. I attribute my ring rust to missing some minor details, but I had covered them elsewhere in the app, so surfacing them for greater discoverability was a simple address. During the Pilot we received positive feedback from many avenues of the business and I was shuffled onto another project to continue the efforts of OPCT and Full Price Clearance tool as I was originally assigned with my new hire designer having really worked hard for the cause extracting information and putting together the requirements gathering and diagraming process as a liasion for the business and Product Management, etc. I’ve been getting a lot of buzz and I am happy with how I joined this effort and how everyone rose to the occasion and even with the pressure we were able to deliver a quality product in record time. I am about to tell everyone thank you for the trust. They all earned mine and I hope that I can continue to show and prove. Again, I am kicking off ring rust and re-entering the work force and it was tough but I have been been elated by the friendships forged in the trenches and managerial, directorial, peer, on up to VP feedback I’ve received.

Set the tone

To design the final mock up we need to set the tones of the app that reflect the brand of the product. Since the app didn’t have this yet, I created quick simple brand guideline to ensure all the design process will follow the guides and become more consistent from wireframing and presenting early to the business to higher fidelity CSS and JavaScript Prototypes to being able to wireframe in the browser using our minimal framework that would join the CommonUI and continue to improve the fit and finish of all our products.

Put the details

As we continued to grow and discover how we could fit everything that needed to be included to satiate the business requirements but continue to grow with the needs we found card interactions, ribbons to show clearance items, even animations for microinteractions like prices being slashed and crossed our and new prices animated in. The entire process was meant to essentially guide the user to simply know where to click and sort of like whack a mole begin “bug bashing” the verification and validation process.

Conclusion

Still reading this? Glad you make it to this point! So I learn few things when designing this app.

This effort would serve for the foundation of our future automation work in machine learning and artificial intel. I am hoping that the system can run this in a detached headless browser state and everyday when the users start they can basically have all the work pre-verified and ready to be price matched and sent to the buying office. I want to create another companion PWA experience where the QAs and BO’s can simply trust the AI and our systems utilize this information to identify trends to determine when to sell high or move inventory around. A lot of insights can be gathered from why inventory is being sold for the low or prices and inventory fluctuate. I am sure it’s already innate and baked into the processes that buyers and the business already strategies around. I am imagining that this new AI will not take away jobs from people but allow them to work harder and smarter and be more effective. Essentially everyone will have a personally assistant, a robot, that takes a lot of the tedious manual labor off their plate and allows them to see the greater landscape for their decision making.

Thank you for reading this! :)

  • Quality matters in all details, big and small.
  • Innovation is for the customer, not for ourselves or the competition.
  • Accessibility for people and devices is a core customer service value.
  • Performance makes or breaks even the most carefully designed experience.
  • Consistency simplifies the customer experience.
  • Effortlessness is as important in design as it is in fashion.
May 2018-Present / Seattle, WA
